A Brief Cultural History of Levantine and Arabian Spices

To understand the significance of regional ingredients in today’s fine dining, we must first step back in time. The Levant and the Arabian Peninsula have long been epicentres of spice trade, culinary innovation, and cultural exchange. Spices weren’t merely condiments—they were currency, medicine, and even power.

From bustling souks in ancient Damascus to the maritime spice routes that threaded through the Red Sea, this region has always been rich in both flavour and story. Ingredients such as cardamom, cinnamon, and cloves were transported in caravans and ships, traded between merchants and rulers, and woven into the fabric of local cuisine. But beyond trade, the cultivation and blending of spices became an art form.

Take za’atar, for example. A blend often made with thyme, sumac, sesame seeds, and salt, it’s more than a seasoning—it’s a cultural icon. Families in the Levant each have their own variation, passed down through generations. To sprinkle za’atar over warm flatbread or fold it into labneh is to partake in a daily ritual deeply embedded in identity.

Similarly, sumac—with its bold, citrusy tang—was used long before lemons were readily available. Its deep maroon hue and sharp notes bring both colour and contrast to dishes, serving both aesthetic and flavour functions. Then there's saffron, one of the most prized spices in the world, often associated with Persian and Arabian culinary traditions. Its golden threads have perfumed rice dishes, sweets, and stews for centuries.

What makes these spices so compelling isn’t just their flavour profile—it’s the memory they evoke. A hint of cardamom might recall a grandmother’s kitchen. The sharpness of sumac could stir memories of a family meal under olive trees. They’re edible artefacts, drawing history into the present with each bite.

Today, chefs in fine dining Abu Dhabi establishments are rediscovering these spices not as relics but as tools to build bridges—between modern techniques and traditional roots, between global influences and regional pride.

Reimagining Traditional Ingredients for a Vegetarian Fine Dining Experience

The perception of vegetarian cuisine has undergone a quiet transformation. Once relegated to the sidelines of haute cuisine, vegetarian dishes are now taking centre stage in fine dining settings. But what makes them sing? The answer lies in how chefs are reimagining traditional ingredients—not just as substitutes, but as stars in their own right.

Regional spices and plant-based staples are now used not only for authenticity but to elevate taste, texture, and complexity. These ingredients, long appreciated in home kitchens across the Middle East, are being refined and reinterpreted with modern techniques and artistic presentation.

Consider aubergine. A staple across Levantine and Arabian cuisine, it’s found in everything from smoky moutabal to stuffed mahshi. In an upscale vegetarian tasting menu, the same aubergine might be slow-roasted, smoked over wood chips, and paired with a drizzle of tahini foam and a dusting of za’atar-infused salt—transforming the familiar into the extraordinary.

Sumac, with its tangy sharpness, might no longer just garnish a salad. It could be infused into an emulsion, reduced into a glaze, or dehydrated into a powder to line the rim of a cocktail glass. Saffron may leave behind its traditional rice partners to perfume an almond milk panna cotta or lace a delicate broth poured tableside over roasted root vegetables.

What sets these reimagined uses apart isn’t just technique, but intention. Fine dining is no longer about adding meat alternatives for the sake of it. It’s about constructing a flavour narrative that honours tradition while embracing innovation. The herbs and spices of the region allow chefs to do exactly that—telling stories that connect the diner with history, without sacrificing culinary sophistication.

Gala in Abu Dhabi is an example of this culinary ethos in action. Dishes are carefully crafted using classic regional ingredients in ways that surprise and delight. And within the growing scene of fine dining Abu Dhabi, it’s clear that this approach is more than a passing trend—it’s a cultural refinement rooted in legacy.

This shift shows that vegetarian fine dining isn’t a compromise—it’s a canvas. And regional ingredients are the brushstrokes that paint the experience.

The Use of Za’atar, Sumac, and Saffron in Upscale Dishes

In the hands of a fine dining chef, regional spices transcend their everyday roles. They’re no longer just sprinkled or stirred—they are deconstructed, reconstructed, and choreographed to play leading roles in refined, multi-sensory experiences. Za’atar, sumac, and saffron are particularly favoured not just for their depth of flavour but for the emotional and visual cues they bring to a dish.

Za’atar: The Earthy Balancer

Za’atar’s wild, herbal character lends itself well to dishes where a grounding element is needed. Its blend of thyme, oregano, toasted sesame, and sumac brings both brightness and richness—an unusual pairing in Western flavour logic, but natural in Middle Eastern cooking.

In fine dining, chefs have taken za’atar beyond the traditional flatbread topping. Imagine a beetroot tartare with za’atar oil, offering an earthy foil to the root vegetable’s sweetness. Or a rosemary-infused sorbet served with a brittle spun from sesame and za’atar, blurring the line between savoury and sweet.

Sumac: The Sharp Contrast

Sumac has become a favourite for adding acidity without moisture. Its lemony tang and deep red hue offer chefs both a flavour lift and a visual accent. In haute cuisine, presentation is as crucial as taste—sumac offers both.

One upscale approach includes sumac-dusted tempura courgette flowers, plated with a swipe of date purée. Another reinvention pairs sumac with grilled peaches and almond cream, bridging traditional flavour profiles with avant-garde combinations. It’s not about surprise for the sake of it, but resonance—the sumac sparks recognition while its usage redefines expectation.

Saffron: The Fragrant Signature

Saffron brings both luxury and complexity. Its cost makes it a statement in any dish, but its power lies in its versatility. Just a few threads infuse broths, custards, or syrups with a distinctively floral warmth that lingers on the palate.

At the fine dining level, saffron might be paired with a roasted parsnip purée under a tower of delicately charred cauliflower florets. It may perfume a chilled almond soup starter, or form the base note in a saffron-cardamom cream nestled beneath citrus segments and fennel pollen.

These three ingredients serve as more than taste profiles—they are signature elements that chefs can reinterpret endlessly. In the world of fine dining Abu Dhabi, their use reflects both innovation and identity, where flavour becomes an instrument of cultural expression. Their cultural weight and sensory impact make them indispensable to vegetarian fine dining menus, offering diners something deeper than novelty: memory, innovation, and a sense of place.

Reviving Old Recipes with Modern Techniques

Modern fine dining does not seek to replace tradition—it reawakens it. The art lies in identifying recipes with deep cultural roots and gently teasing them into contemporary forms. Through culinary technique, presentation, and storytelling, chefs are finding new ways to make the old feel timeless.

The Role of Preservation and Precision

Many traditional Middle Eastern and Levantine recipes were designed for sustenance, passed orally with vague measurements and abundant improvisation. Today’s chefs bring precision to these foundations, carefully measuring pH levels in fermented yoghurt sauces or calibrating sous vide temperatures for slow-cooked legumes. This attention doesn’t sterilise tradition—it honours it through scientific respect.

Take mujaddara, a dish of lentils, rice, and caramelised onions. In an upscale vegetarian tasting menu, the lentils might be slow-cooked in spiced vegetable stock, the onions caramelised to an exact stage where sweetness balances bitterness, and the rice transformed into a crisped wafer for texture contrast. A drizzle of infused oil—perhaps sumac or za’atar—brings aromatic brightness to the plate.

Plating the Past with the Future

One of the most notable changes in modern reinterpretations is presentation. Traditional dishes that once arrived family-style in clay pots are now plated with intention, often using asymmetry, negative space, and layered textures. A tabbouleh reinterpretation may come as a compressed cucumber roll filled with bulgur and herbs, adorned with dots of pomegranate molasses and microgreens.

A hummus may no longer be a simple dip—it might be whipped and aerated, piped into a spiral nest holding roasted chickpeas, olive gel, and slivers of preserved lemon. These visual choices turn the familiar into theatre, where every element earns its place.

Technology Meets Tradition

Technology is also pushing boundaries. Dehydrators intensify flavours without additives, while smoke guns and liquid nitrogen can introduce unexpected textures or aromas that elevate a dish’s profile. Traditional ingredients like date syrup, tamarind, or rose water are being reimagined through these tools—not altered, but enhanced.

This revival is especially evident across the fine dining Abu Dhabi scene, where chefs are not just curating meals but curating memory. Notably, the UAE has witnessed a 34% increase in demand for vegan and vegetarian offerings in high-end restaurants over the past two years—a trend that has encouraged greater experimentation with heritage-based plant-forward cuisine. By revisiting ancestral recipes with a modern eye, chefs bridge generations. Diners are no longer just consuming food—they’re participating in a conversation between the past and the present. Each bite is an invitation to reflect, recognise, and revel in the evolution of culinary heritage.

In the fine dining experience, especially within vegetarian and vegan cuisine, it is not enough for food to simply taste good—it must resonate. Regional spices like za’atar, sumac, and saffron do more than season a plate. They connect diners to centuries of tradition, to stories whispered in kitchens and marketplaces, and to flavours that evoke belonging even across borders.

As chefs reimagine these ingredients through modern techniques and artistic expression, they do more than innovate—they honour. They revive forgotten recipes, enhance them with precise craftsmanship, and present them in forms that delight both the palate and the eyes. Gala in Abu Dhabi is one such venue showcasing how these traditional flavours can be given elegant, contemporary form—but the principle extends far beyond any single restaurant.

For British diners and culinary professionals alike, there’s inspiration in how heritage and high-end hospitality can coexist. It challenges the notion that innovation must abandon the past. Instead, we see that the richest expressions of creativity often come not from invention, but from reinterpretation.
